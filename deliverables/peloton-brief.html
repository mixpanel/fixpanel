POC Dataset

What we built for you

we took your Amplitude governance export and used it to build a synthetic dataset that mirrors your real product. schema-matched synthetic data that preserves the structure and complexity of your instrumentation without touching production. this dataset is running in a Mixpanel project today and will be the foundation for the POC evaluation.

Event curation

starting from 6,629 active events, we applied a systematic filter: removed 4,865 zero-volume events, removed events under 100K monthly volume, removed internal/system events (OTA updates, hardware diagnostics, packet errors, sensor pairing, calibration), and consolidated redundant UI micro-events while keeping representative samples.

200 user-facing events across 18 categories

covering discovery, workout lifecycle, in-class engagement, social interactions, challenges, search, notifications, subscriptions, and device context. every event name matches your real Amplitude instrumentation. when your team opens Mixpanel, the taxonomy will feel familiar.

POC Framework

What this means for the POC

the POC success criteria (shared separately) are organized around three drivers: cost (Greg's focus on right-sizing the analytics spend), migration lift (Tahamina's concern about engineering capacity and execution timeline), and incremental value over your current setup (Neha's goal of unblocking self-serve for PMs).

Day-one schema compatibility

every event in the POC dataset uses your exact Amplitude event names and property names. when your team opens Mixpanel, they'll see Started Workout, Viewed Class Details, Finalized Workout, Joined Challenge, not a Mixpanel-invented taxonomy. from our point of view, migration is a configuration exercise, not a re-instrumentation project.

Self-serve for PMs

Neha's team is currently a bottleneck for analytical requests from PMs and execs. the POC will test whether Becca, with no prior Mixpanel training, can build a funnel from content recommendation to workout completion, segmented by member tenure, in under 15 minutes.

Becca has also raised a specific frustration with Amplitude's retention charts: the re-entry behavior, where a user returning in both week 1 and week 2 gets counted in ways that distort the cohort. Mixpanel's retention uses true calendar bucketing with single-count-per-bucket by default. the POC dataset is designed to let Becca test this directly, including fixed-date cohort locking applied as a filter on retention and reports.

feature engagement reports for Peloton IQ, Club Peloton, Teams, and Strength+ should be buildable by a PM without a ticket to the data team. Neha specifically wants to see Metric Trees for mapping KPI hierarchies and Spark AI for natural language queries from non-technical stakeholders.

Schema evolution over time

this is where Mixpanel's approach differs. the 200 curated events and the full 6,629 can coexist. Mixpanel's event management lets you hide, merge, and remap events through configuration, not code changes.

The evolution path

  • week one: everything works with your existing event names. no cleanup required.
  • over time: hide low-value events, enforce property types through Lexicon, normalize corrupted property names (Device Langu㴺 remaps to Device Language), strip Segment metadata that adds noise without adding insight.
  • the cleanup is gradual, non-destructive, and doesn't require engineering capacity. Tahamina, this means the migration timeline isn't gated on engineering sprints for schema cleanup. the analytics team can evolve the schema on their own schedule while engineering focuses on the Segment destination swap.

Group analytics for subscription-level analysis

Peloton's business has a natural hierarchy that most product analytics tools ignore: the subscription. a single Subscription ID can cover multiple users on a family plan. one person pays, two or three people ride. the account-level questions that matter (which family plans retain best? do households where multiple members are active churn less? what's the conversion rate from single-user to family plan?) live at the subscription level, not the user level.

in Amplitude, this analysis isn't possible in the product. Subscription ID is a property on events, but Amplitude can't group, funnel, or retain by it. every time your team needs to answer a subscription-level question, the workflow is: export to BigQuery, write SQL, aggregate, and report back. that's a data engineering ticket, a wait, and a one-off deliverable that nobody can iterate on.

Example: family plan churn analysis

the question: of family plan subscriptions where only one member is active in a given month, what percentage churn within 90 days versus subscriptions where two or more members are active?
in Amplitude: BigQuery pipeline, per-member activity counts, survival analysis in a notebook. a week of work, minimum.
in Mixpanel: define Subscription ID as a Group key. build a behavioral cohort. drop it into a retention report. minutes, self-serve, and anyone on the team can modify the query without writing SQL.

the POC dataset includes subscription-level groupings so your team can test this workflow directly.

Experimentation and data science

your engineering team has a strong preference for Optimizely, and we're not asking you to change that. Mixpanel can ingest Optimizely exposure events and compute lift and statistical significance as the analysis layer, without replacing Optimizely for feature deployment. the POC includes simulated experiment data to test this workflow end to end.

Aly, on the statistical rigor question: Mixpanel's experiment reports use sequential testing with false discovery rate controls and display a confidence banner that explicitly tells users not to peek at results before statistical significance is reached. the POC will let you compare this against your current Optimizely analysis workflow. Mixpanel also enforces Lexicon-level governance (event descriptions, property types, visibility controls) that guides non-technical users toward the correct events.

Signal Reports will run against the synthetic data to surface behavioral correlations with retention, the kind of analysis that currently requires writing Python scripts from scratch.
